The Michigan Student Artificial Intelligence Lab (MSAIL) is a student organization for discussion of artificial intelligence and machine learning. Andrew Ng said:

“ ...if you read research papers consistently, if you seriously study half a dozen papers a week and you do that for two years, after those two years you will have learned a lot... But that sort of investment, if you spend a whole Saturday studying rather than watching TV, there's no one there to pat you on the back or tell you you did a good job. ”  —  Andrew Ng
MSAIL is a community in which motivated students can read and discuss modern machine learning literature together. We welcome students of all backgrounds and ability. To join MSAIL and stay up to date, simply join our Slack team! Also be sure to check out our sister organization: the Michigan Data Science Team! We are both graciously sponsored by the Michigan Institute for Data Science.

Reading Group Green AI
Tuesday (2019-12-03) at 18:00 in BBB 3725

Computations required for deep learning research have been doubling every few months, resulting in an estimated 300,000x increase from 2012 to 2018. Training a single AI model can emit as much carbon as five cars in their lifetime. In this paper, the Allen Institute hopes to make the distinction between research solely focused on improving results (coined Red AI) and those that improve upon results without additional computational power required (coined Green AI). This week we discussed the benefits of making efficiency a more common evaluation criterion for AI papers alongside accuracy and related measures. We deduced it is important to increase the prevalence of Green AI so AI development will be accessible to everyone not just large corporations and research institutions.

TUE
Nov 19
Reading Group Annotator Bias in Natural Language Understanding Datasets
Tuesday (2019-11-19) at 18:00 in BBB 3725

In the natural language processing field (chatbots, question-answering, etc), researchers often curate large datasets of labelled text from crowdworkers, or people who get paid to label data. A common crowdsourcing practice is to recruit a small number of high-quality workers, and have them massively generate examples. Having only a few workers generate data can result in skewed data representative of the lack of data diversity. This is a big problem when crowdworkers are freely generating sentences. This paper performs a series of experiments showing these concerns are evident in three recent NLP datasets. These findings suggest that annotator bias should be monitored during dataset creation, and that test set annotators should be disjoint from training set annotators.
